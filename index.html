Paper Code

Earlier UMM studies generally report that scaling up the amount and duration of understanding training improves generation benchmark performance (GenEval, ...), and vice versa (MMMU...). However, such broad benchmark scores cannot tell us whether a specific skill — like counting — is shared between image understanding and generation. We look at this directly, one capability at a time.

The Question

In a UMM, if we sharpen a specific capability — say counting — on the understanding task alone, does generation of that capability improve too? And does it work in reverse?

Our Finding

Yes — but it depends on the architecture. In our experiments, such capability sharing (transferability) emerges most clearly on model which use shared transformer with an unified visual encoder.

ARCHITECTURE & TRANSFER

Architecture Analysis : In which UMM architecture does transfer emerge?


Def. Of Transferability

Train a specific capability (e.g., counting) on single task (understanding or generation), then check whether the same capability improves on the other task — without ever training it there directly.

Takeaway

We empirically observe that transfer appears most clearly in architecture (a) — a shared backbone with a unified encoder — while other decoupled designs show little or no transfer. This may suggest that sharing trasnformer backbone & input image representations helps capabilities transfer between tasks.

Experimental Settings

We select open-source model from each architecture family and evaluate their transferability. We consider two transfer directions. (1) und→gen : Train on understanding, test on generation. (2) gen→und : Train on generation, test on understanding.

PRACTICAL RECIPE

Can we add a generative capability without hurting image quality?

The Problem

Want to boost a generative skill? Fine-tuning generation directly pulls the model toward the training set — a distribution shift that can degrade image quality.

The Recipe

Train understanding, not generation. In our experiments this improves the target generative skill while keeping the distribution — and image quality — closer to the original.

Takeaway

und→gen (Transfer) can improve target generative capabilities while keeping image quality closer to the baseline (lower FID). Direct training achieves better accuracy, but image distribution drifts much higher from the baseline (higher FID).

DISCUSSIONS

Is und→gen always the stronger direction?

We measure transfer strength as the improvement from transfer relative to direct training — e.g., 150% means transfer achieved 1.5× the improvement of direct training.

Takeaway #2

Not always. In our Lumina-DiMOO experiments, und→gen tends to be stronger for counting and spatial relation, while gen→und tends to be stronger for text. We hypothesize this is because text depends on fine-grained, pixel-level detail — which the generation objective may capture more directly than understanding does.

Does existence of transferability guarantee simultaneous gains on joint training?

Takeaway

Not necessarily. When we trained und+gen together, the two did not improve at the same time — understanding rose while generation fell, then the trend reversed. This suggests that a transfer path existing doesn’t guarantee gains in both directions at once.
